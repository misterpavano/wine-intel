Brettanomyces / SO2 Resistance

Wine Australia Research: Brettanomyces Strains Actively Evolving Sulfite Tolerance — Multi-Tool Control Now Essential

Active research from Wine Australia confirms that Brettanomyces bruxellensis strains are evolving greater sulfite tolerance, with evidence of this occurring in commercial winery environments. The research highlights that SO2-only control strategies are increasingly inadequate, and that multi-hurdle approaches — combining SO2 with chitosan, lysozyme, fumaric acid, and physical interventions — deliver more reliable protection. Monitoring Brett in viable-but-non-culturable (VBNC) states remains a critical challenge, as standard plating methods miss these populations entirely.
💡 What this means
The tool that used to work — add SO2, done — is becoming less reliable as the organism evolves. Wineries still relying on sulfites alone as their brett management strategy are at growing risk, especially at higher wine pH where SO2 molecular fraction drops. The science is now backing what suppliers have been recommending for years: layered protection works, single-tool approaches fail.
✅ Your takeaway
This research is your opening with any account still using SO2-only brett programs. Print the Wine Australia finding and walk in with it: "There's new data showing brett is developing sulfite tolerance in commercial wineries. What does your current brett protocol look like beyond SO2?" Then walk them through Enartis's chitosan and complementary stability tools — the data now backs the ask.

Detection Technology / Brett Monitoring

Breakthrough Flow Cytometry Method Enables Real-Time Brettanomyces Monitoring in Wine — Including VBNC States

A new analytical method using antibodies, magnetic beads, and flow cytometry allows winemakers to detect Brettanomyces in its viable-but-non-culturable (VBNC) state — the dormant form that conventional plating misses entirely and that can reactivate under cellar conditions. The method provides real-time metabolic activity data, enabling faster, more informed intervention decisions before brett populations reach detectable sensory thresholds. This represents a significant advance over traditional culture-based monitoring.
💡 What this means
Most brett problems are caught too late because conventional testing can't see VBNC populations. By the time culture-based tests return a positive, brett has often already produced ethyl phenols. Real-time flow cytometry monitoring closes that gap — but it needs to be paired with responsive intervention tools to act on what the monitoring reveals.
✅ Your takeaway
Technical winemakers investing in advanced brett monitoring want suppliers who can keep up with the protocol demands that real-time detection creates. Ask tech-forward accounts whether they're using or considering advanced brett monitoring — and then position Enartis's chitosan and targeted intervention tools as the response layer. Monitoring without fast intervention capability is half a solution.

SO2 Alternatives / Research Update

OIV-Approved Chitosan and Fumaric Acid Gaining Traction as Practical SO2 Alternatives in Commercial Winemaking

Peer-reviewed research published in MDPI Fermentation confirms that OIV-approved chitosan (effective against Brettanomyces, LAB, and acetic acid bacteria) and fumaric acid (inhibiting MLF, preserving malic acidity) are establishing themselves as practical SO2 reduction tools in commercial winemaking — particularly for high-pH wines where SO2 molecular fraction is insufficient. Insect-derived chitosan is also being explored as a sustainable alternative. The research reinforces a hurdle-concept approach: no single tool replaces SO2, but combinations achieve comparable protection with lower additive loads.
💡 What this means
OIV approval matters: it's the global scientific endorsement that gives winemakers confidence to use these tools in regulated markets. Chitosan and fumaric acid are no longer fringe additions — they're mainstream stability options with a clear regulatory green light and a growing body of supporting research.
✅ Your takeaway
If you're not already leading Enartis chitosan conversations with high-pH accounts (Merlot, Grenache, warm-climate reds), start now. The OIV approval and peer-reviewed backing remove the hesitation most winemakers had two years ago. A simple ask works: "Your pH is running high this vintage — have you tried chitosan as part of your brett protocol?" The science supports the conversation.
