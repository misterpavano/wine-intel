Smoke Taint / Flash Détente Surge

Smoke Taint Pressure Drives Unusual Flash Détente Demand in California — New Adopters Entering Mid-Crush Without a Program

Mobile flash détente operators — led by Winesecrets in Sebastopol — are reporting growing demand from wineries that had not previously used heat extraction technology but turned to it in 2025 as a smoke taint mitigation tool. Flash détente's vacuum-cooling process volatilizes and evacuates smoke-derived guaiacol and cresol compounds before alcoholic fermentation, making it one of the most effective technical interventions available for taint-compromised fruit. The challenge: many first-time adopters arrive without an established fermentation input program for heat-extracted musts, creating an urgent need for supplier-level technical guidance at crush.
💡 What this means
Flash détente creates fundamentally different must chemistry: inactivated native microflora, elevated temperatures, altered tannin-polysaccharide structure, and modified nutrient profiles. The yeasts, enzymes, and nutrients that work well in conventionally crushed wine often underperform in heat-extracted musts. First-time adopters learning this mid-fermentation are a supplier opportunity that requires technical credibility — not just catalog support.
✅ Your takeaway
Ask every red wine account in your territory: "Did smoke taint touch any of your blocks last harvest, and did you use flash détente?" If they used it without a specific input program, walk them through what Enartis recommends for heat-extracted musts — yeast selection for temperature tolerance, enzyme programs for polysaccharide extraction, nutrient management for the altered nitrogen environment. Being the rep with a specific protocol ready for flash détente is a real differentiator in smoke-affected regions.

OIV Research / Thermovinification Enzymes

New OIV-Backed Research: Flash Détente Musts Require Distinct Enzyme Programs to Fully Unlock Polysaccharide Benefit

Emerging OIV-supported research confirms that the polysaccharide-rich matrix created by flash détente heat treatment requires specifically selected pectolytic enzymes — not conventional maceration enzymes — to fully release arabinogalactans and rhamnogalacturonans from the thermally altered cell walls. Standard enzyme programs designed for cold maceration are less effective on flash détente musts because the heat treatment pre-breaks cell wall structure differently, often requiring enzymes with higher temperature stability and different substrate affinity profiles. The finding has direct implications for winemakers combining flash détente with premium extraction goals.
💡 What this means
If a winemaker is using flash détente but running the same enzyme program they use for cold maceration, they're leaving polysaccharide complexity and textural quality on the table. The heat-altered must matrix is a different substrate, and "one enzyme fits all" doesn't hold for thermovinification. This is a technical insight most enzyme suppliers haven't communicated clearly — yet.
✅ Your takeaway
Ask flash détente users what enzyme program they're running. If the answer is "same as always," you've identified an upgrade conversation. Bring the technical brief and walk through how heat-altered cell walls respond differently to enzymatic hydrolysis. Selling a better enzyme program to an existing customer who's already committed to flash détente is higher-yield than finding new adopters. This is a retention and upgrade story.

Microbial Stability
Brettanomyces / VBNC States

Brett Is Surviving Your Sulfite Program — VBNC Populations Detected After Standard SO2 Additions in Commercial Cellars

Ongoing Wine Australia research into Brettanomyces bruxellensis is confirming what forward-looking winemakers have suspected: standard SO2 additions are leaving viable-but-non-culturable (VBNC) Brett populations intact in barrels and tanks. These dormant cells are undetectable by conventional plating methods but retain the ability to reactivate when cellar conditions shift — particularly during spring temperature swings when barrel room temps rise. The research is reshaping how wineries think about "effective" brett control: a negative culture result does not mean a clean cellar.
💡 What this means
The standard quality control workflow — sulfite, plate, pass — is giving false confidence. VBNC Brett populations survive at molecular SO2 levels that winemakers trust are lethal. When the barrel room warms up, those populations activate and produce ethyl phenols before any monitoring system catches them. The only reliable defense is a multi-hurdle program that addresses Brett biology beyond the reach of molecular SO2.
✅ Your takeaway
Use this research as the conversation starter with any account that reports brett issues despite correct SO2 management. "You're doing everything right and still getting brett? Here's why." The VBNC mechanism explains the failure, and chitosan is the intervention that works at the VBNC stage — it disrupts cell wall integrity even in dormant populations. Lead with the science, close with the product.

Chitosan and Fumaric Acid Advancing Beyond EU — OIV Approval Enabling Global Use in Export Markets

OIV-approved chitosan (effective against Brettanomyces, LAB, and acetic acid bacteria) and fumaric acid (MLF inhibition for malic acid preservation) are now being incorporated into winemaking regulations beyond the EU — their OIV approval status making them exportable tools for North American wineries producing for international markets. 💡 What this means

Inline Brix, Temperature, and Phenolic Sensors Now Commercially Deployed at Mid-Size Wineries — Real-Time Fermentation Decisions Replace Daily Lab Pulls

Inline fermentation monitoring — combining continuous Brix, temperature, dissolved CO2, and in-tank phenolic analysis — is moving from large-scale winery installations into mid-size producers (5,000 to 50,000 cases). Platforms like Enologix, iOEnology, and vineyard-linked SCADA systems are increasingly common at tech-forward accounts. The shift from twice-daily manual lab samples to continuous data streams is fundamentally changing when and why winemakers adjust their fermentation inputs — creating both a challenge and an opportunity for suppliers who can speak the data language.
💡 What this means

"Add nutrient at 1/3 sugar depletion" is too generic for someone watching real-time Brix curves. The winemakers investing in inline monitoring are, by definition, the ones who want precision — and they'll respond to suppliers who can quantify outcomes, not just describe protocols.
✅ Your takeaway
Ask your tech-forward accounts whether they're running inline monitoring, and if so, what metrics they track. Use their own data in your recommendations: "Given where your Brix is at hour 48, here's what the nutrient timing looks like with Enartis Nutriferm Advance." Matching your recommendation cadence to their monitoring cadence — real-time, data-specific — is how you become the supplier they trust for complex fermentations.
